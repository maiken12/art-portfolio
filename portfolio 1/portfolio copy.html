01. DRAWING: LINE & MUSIC

Art Lesson Plan Idea (Pre-K)

Goal: Students will learn that lines can show sound, movement, and feeling in art.

Objectives:

  • Students will make different kinds of lines, such as straight, curved, wavy, and zigzag.
  • Students will listen to music and create lines that match what they hear.
  • Students will move their bodies in ways that match how they think their lines would move.
  • Students will use oil pastels, watercolor paint, and a spray bottle to explore how different materials can change and move across the paper.

Description: This lesson helps Pre-K students explore how music, movement, and art can connect. The teacher shows children that artists can use lines to express sound, feeling, and motion. Students look at an artwork and talk about the different lines they notice. Then, while listening to music, students can first move their bodies in ways that match the kinds of lines they hear. After moving, students use oil pastels to draw lines inspired by the music. Next, they paint over their lines with watercolor and observe how the oil pastel resists the paint. The teacher can also add a small spray bottle step, where students gently spray water onto more concentrated watercolor to watch the colors spread and move around the page. At the end, students share their artwork and talk about how the music, movement, and materials helped them create their lines.

Reflection

This project helped me personally see how closely music, movement, and art can work together. As I listened to the music, I found myself responding to different sounds by making zig zags, continuous loops, short lines, and curved lines with the oil pastels. Some parts of the music felt more sudden or strong, which made me create zig zags and short lines, while other parts felt softer and more flowing, which led me to make continuous loops and curved lines. I also liked using oil pastels because they made the lines feel bold and easy to see. One part of the project that stood out to me was adding watercolor over the oil pastel lines. I liked seeing how the watercolor moved over the paper while the oil pastel resisted it and stayed visible underneath. Once the music started, I did not have to think as hard about what to draw because the sounds seemed to guide my hand. The rhythm and changes in the music made it easier for me to move from one type of line to another. I also think the movement part of this lesson would be very helpful for young children.

02. COLLAGE: SHAPES

Art Lesson Plan Idea (Pre-K)

Goal: Students will learn that shapes can be made in many ways by changing paper with their hands and scissors.

Objectives:

  • Students will explore making shapes by tearing paper.
  • Students will cut different kinds of lines (straight, curved, zigzag) to build new shapes.
  • Students will notice that folding and cutting can create both a shape and an open space.

Description: Students explore paper as a flexible material. They practice tearing paper to create organic shapes and using scissors to cut different kinds of lines (straight, curved, zigzag) to build geometric or abstract shapes. The teacher introduces the concept of positive and negative space by showing how folding and cutting can create both a physical shape and an open space on the page. Students then arrange and glue their created shapes onto a background paper to form a unique composition.

Reflection

I think this assignment was far the most accessible art project we have worked on so far. While working on some of the other art project assignments I found myself trying to make my work “perfect”, but in this case, “perfection” was not possible. I felt like there was no right or wrong way to rip a piece of paper, especially because the prompt was pretty open ended. I think this project would be great for students as young as two-years-old, it supports fine motor skill development as well as creativity, learning about shapes and lines, and introduces new vocabulary, mainly adjectives. I would be interested to see how adjusting this kind of lesson for a range of ages would look.

03. VIRTUAL FIELD TRIP

Observations & Process

I really enjoyed the tour Joyce gave. I was unable to attend a live event, so I viewed the pre-recorded tour and talk. She gave an insightful history of Jackson Pollock’s workspace, his history as an artist, and background on his life. I was a history major in college. Part of the reason I loved studying history was learning about the stories behind people. Joyce kept me engaged by pointing at specific items, like the "strange thing in the barn" (a skull), and asking thought-provoking questions like "How did Pollock know what to do?", "What feelings do you think this shows?", "What is happening in this picture?", and "How would you be able to tell he's screaming?"

Reflection

I really enjoyed making my Pollock inspired piece of art, I actually decided to do something similar with my two-year-old students after I made my own. There is something very forgiving about an artistic process that doesn't have any limitations. I found it hard to pour the paint exactly where I wanted it to go, so I tried a few different techniques of pouring the paint. I put some paint in a plastic sandwich bag and cut off the corner, like a piping bag for cake decorating, and squeezed it out of the bag with some more control. I also experimented with holding a cup of paint at different heights and angles to control the placement of the paint and the thickness of each blob of paint.

04. MANDALA

Art Lesson Plan Idea (Pre-K / 4s)

Goal: Students will create a self-portrait mandala that represents themselves as part of a family, using colors, shapes, and repeated patterns to show important people, feelings, and connections in their lives.

Objectives:

  • Students will identify themselves as a member of a family and reflect on the people who are important to them.
  • Students will use mirrors and observation skills to create a simple self-portrait showing facial features, hair, and skin tone.
  • Students will explore mandala-inspired art by using repeated shapes, lines, colors, and symbols around their self-portrait to represent family members, relationships, and things they enjoy doing together.
  • Students will describe their artwork using words, drawings, and conversation to share ideas about their family.

Description: This lesson connects to the Family Study and supports Social-Emotional Development. The teacher begins by discussing families, emphasizing that every family is unique. Students look in mirrors and create a self-portrait in the center of a paper. Around the outside of their face, they add repeated shapes, colors, and symbols to represent their family members and experiences. For example, hearts for love or repeated colors that remind them of home. At the end, students share their portraits in small groups, describing what the shapes and colors in their mandala mean.

Reflection

I really enjoyed working on my mandala, I had always thought that mandalas were always supposed to be 100% symmetrical, and found it interesting to learn about different kinds of mandalas. For my mandala, I followed the instructions closely, until I felt as though I had a strong enough grasp on the process that I could add my own creativity through color and different designs to my mandala. I could feel myself getting into a very relaxed state as I got more comfortable. I was surprised, typically, I tend to be hypercritical of my own artwork. I think that the relaxing nature of this assignment would be great for my students, particularly ones that struggle with enjoying the process.

05. PUPPETS & SCRIPT

Art Lesson Plan Idea: Paper Bag Puppet Emotions

Goal: Students will create a puppet that expresses a specific emotion, connecting literacy to visual self-expression.

Objectives:

  • Students will identify and discuss different emotions introduced in a book.
  • Students will use various materials to create a puppet representing a specific feeling.
  • Students will share and explain the features they included on their puppet to represent their chosen emotion.

Description: The teacher does a read aloud with a book that discusses emotions. After the read aloud, each student will be given materials (paper bags, markers, yarn, paper) to make their own “paper bag puppet emotion.” After the students are done, the teacher will gather the class and have students share their puppets, explaining the emotion they chose and the features they used.

Reflection

I really enjoyed making my puppet, “Ms. Maple”! I decided to make my puppet look on the outside the way I wanted her to come across in her personality, colorful and very aware of her surroundings. I made her eyes very wide, added funky, colorful hair, and a heart on her pink shirt to illustrate this. The actual performance went well; we had some technical constraints with green screens, but I was pleasantly surprised at how simple it was to perform the puppet show in a virtual setting. I like how there were independent elements of this project as well as group work.

06. PAINTING

Art Lesson Idea (Pre-K)

Goal: Students will learn to recognize, sort, and name colors.

Objectives:

  • Students will identify primary colors and classroom objects of those colors.
  • Students will sort objects by color into groups.
  • Students will observe and describe how mixing primary colors creates new colors.

Description: The teacher introduces colors through a "Color Hunt" where children find classroom objects that match specific colors. After collecting objects, students help build a shared class color chart. The teacher also demonstrates mixing primary colors using tempera paint, allowing students to observe the changes in real-time.

Reflection

I was surprised to see how much harder it was to use tempera paint than it was to use acrylics. I also noticed that blue, red, and yellow produced a maroon color instead of brown. Overall, making this chart was very relaxing and I really liked the outcome.

COME TOGETHER AND DANCE

Art Lesson Idea: NYC Transportation Sound Map (Age 2-3)

Goal: Students will learn that different modes of transportation make different sounds, and they can represent those sounds through lines, shapes, and marks to create a simple “transportation story” drawing.

Objectives:

  • Students will listen for differences in transportation sounds (loud/quiet, fast/slow, stop/go).
  • Students will create different marks to match what they hear (long lines, short taps, circles, zigzags, dots).
  • Students will connect sounds to specific transportation types (subway/train, bus, car/truck) and describe their artwork using simple words and gestures.

Description: The teacher introduces three transportation “sound scenes” (subway, bus, car) using instruments or objects. Students listen to each scene and draw the sounds using marks that match (e.g., drum roll for train whoosh). The class then creates a “reading” of the transportation trip by placing drawings in order and describing the journey through movement and sound words.

Reflection

Making this piece felt like turning the picture in my head into sound using random things I had around me. When I listened to “Brother, Catch Me If You Can,” I kept imagining a festival where people are holding hands, dancing in big circles, and twirling around each other. I played around with a pen cap, pens, a Diet Coke can, and an HDMI cord to see what each one could do. I started with a steady rhythm for the beginning of the dance, then switched to something arhythmic to match twirling and breaking off. I ended with a quieter sound by running lip balm around the rim of the Diet Coke can. Overall, this made me realize how you can tell a story through sound without any words, just by changing rhythm, volume, and the way the sound feels. My piece is basically my version of the circle dance I imagined while listening to the song.

08. PORTFOLIO REFLECTION

As I look back on the work I have produced this semester, I realize that this course has been as much about my own personal growth as it has been about evolving as an educator. Coming into this class, I often found myself stuck in a "hypercritical" mindset, focused on whether my artwork was "perfect." However, the most rewarding part of this journey has been taking these projects directly into my own classroom. I have absolutely loved using some of the projects I have worked on this semester with my students; my ability to teach them in such a creative way is so exciting for my students and me.

I now see how important it is to provide my students with "limit-free" artistic environments. Whether it is through a Family Mandala that celebrates their unique identities or a Transportation Sound Map, I want my classroom to be a place where "perfection" is not the goal. I am leaving this course not just with a portfolio of work, but with the joy of knowing that I can inspire my students to find their own creative voices just as I have found mine.
